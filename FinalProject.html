Getting Data¶

Nasdaq website.

The Nasdaq is an American stock exchange. It is the second-largest exchange in the world by market capitalization. Nasdaq has a market capitalization of \$7 trillion while the NYSE has one of \$21 trillion. A stock exchange is a place where securities are bought and sold; think of a food market, where vendors can bring their harvests and sell them to the general public. Except instead of food, companies can bring shares of their stock to the market through an initial public offering (IPO).

The Nasdaq is a major player, and has been even from the beginning. When it began trading on February 8, 1971, it was the world's first electronic stock market. At first, it did not have the capability to perform electronic trades, and could only act as a quotation system for prices. Now it processes terabytes of data every day, and has been extremely involved in analytics, recently proposing to launch an options-data service called the "Intellicator Analytic Tool." Some people on Wall Street say it will expose trade algorithms through reverse-engineering, however, read the <a href "https://www.wsj.com/articles/could-the-intellicator-spill-the-markets-secrets-1510223403">Wall Street Journal article</a> on it and form your own opinion!

The Nasdaq website provides up to 10 years of daily historical stock prices and volumes for each stock. It is free to retrieve data from and very easy to use. We will be using this website to download the opening, closing, high, and low prices, along with the volume, for each equity we are interested in. Right now, we are interested in four different companies:

This website provides up to 10 years of daily historical stock prices and volumes for each stock. We will be using this website to download the opening, closing, volume, high, and low prices for each equity we are interested in. Right now, we are interested in four different companies:

  • Exelixis, Inc. (EXEL)
  • ImmunoGen, Inc. (IMGN)

We will walk you through step-by-step how to get this data in the form of a CSV or TSV so that we can analyze it.

Step 1¶

  • Go to the Nasdaq website listed above.
  • Type in which company you'd like to query data from (in our case, we chose EXEL).
  • Press "Submit"

Step 2¶

  • Step 1 will bring you to a recent stock prices page. It includes all the opening and closing prices, as well as the most recent closing time (16:00 time).
  • Click on the dropdown menu next to "Select the Timeframe," and choose what you like. We chose 10 Years.
  • A table of all the values you want is displayed in this timeframe. Scroll to the bottom.

Step 3¶

  • Click on the link that says "Download this file in Excel Format" in the lower right hand corner of the text box.
  • The download screen should pop up. Instead of saving as an excel document, though, save as the following:
  • NAMEHistoricalText.tsv
  • This saves the file in TSV-form, which stands for Tab-Separated Values, an example of delimiter-sepatated values.
  • Use the directory where you saved the .tsv file for the following uploads.

And there you have it! You have a data set. Now, time to get a working data frame from it.

'How much data are we processing?' - we might ask ourselves. Above, we see that EXEL has listed 2,519 row entries in its dataframe. That may not seem like a lot of data in the grand scheme of data analysis, (and in fact, it is a good amount for good analysis) but for visualization of our data, it might get a bit muddled.

We can cut down on the amount of data we are viewing by the date, to make it so that we are only viewing the most recent data. We queried for these values on December 11th, 2017, so we are going to see if we can get only the stock information from the past month.

DATA.copy() makes a copy of a dataframe that was previously computed. We do this so that we do not edit the original dataframe if we need it later.

smallDATA['date'] gets the column from the dataframe smalldata, and represents it as a panda series.

smallDATA[smallDATA['date'] >= 'YYYY-MM-DD'] sets the previous date column to only the dates that are after the date we specify.

Because this sets the dates of those that do not qualify equal to NaT (or, Not-A-Time), we must remove those. We can remove those rows with smallDATA[pd.notnull{smallDATA['date'])].

We want to get rid of the seconds that also are added when we cut the dates down. We cut off the extra '00:00:00' time by running smallDATA['date'].map(lambda t: t.strftime('%Y-%m-%d')). This will enact a specific formula across all parts of a pandas series. In other terms, it uses the MAP part of MAPREDUCE, if you are familiar with Hadoop or OCaml, to implement a function across all parts of a data series.

We will display this data in full so that you can see how much smaller this dataframe is.

Introduction to Analysis¶

There are many different ways in which to analyze stock. One of my favorites is just a simple linear regression analysis, considering most of these stock graphs look like they can have a line of best fit. Let's look into first, doing a linear regression analysis on these data sets, and then fitting a linear regression model onto each plot.

First, we start off with the linear regression analysis. We will be doing this by focusing on the high data for simplicity. You can change this to be the low, or the opening price for the day to fit your comfort.

In order to do this, we will use the smaller data set from the last graph for EXEL to introduce the idea of linear regression analysis, and then do it for the rest of our data.

Effects of Company Events on Stock¶

We've researched ten different events for every company, and now we want to test and see if these events cause changes in the stock. Our hypothesis in the beginning is that it doesn't. We will use different data points from these times for each company to see what effect these events have on stock, on average. An example of what our hand-made data looks like is provided below.

Biotechnology firms go through several stages of research and development to create the pharmaceuticals that might one day be available to the public.

They go through several phases, all of which are important and all of which that bring their own information for shareholders.

The first 4 we will explain here:

Phase 1 trials test for safety. The drug is given to healthy volunteers to determine the drugs most frequent side effects and how it is absorbed and excreted. Normally, 20 to 80 people participate in phase 1 trials.

Phase 2 Does the drug work? Providing the drug is approved for further study, phase 2 is testing for effectiveness along with further safety monitoring. Does the drug have a clinically significant outcome for the condition its meant to treat? Participants include those with the disease or condition that the drug is indicated for. This is where some participants receive the active drug while others receive a placebo or other drug. Participants range from a few dozen to more than 300.

Phase 3 Is it better than current treatments? The FDA and the drugs sponsor work together to design the phase 3 studies. They might study the drug on different populations at different dosages, and in combination with other drugs. These are often double-blind studies where neither the participants nor researchers know who received the active drug. Phase 3 trials are large in scopesometimes involving tens of thousands of people around the world but according to the FDA, most are not larger than 3,000. If the drug is found to benefit the patient more than other drugs, and the side effects are manageable, a new drug application is filed.

Phase 4 trials are sometimes conducted to study effects that emerge once the drug is approved and gains widespread use. If a side effect is reported in a significant number of patients, a phase 4 study might seek to determine if the side effect is connected to the drug or from the drug in combination with another, or certain foods.

Bigger Data Set¶

Part Two

This part introduces the idea of timing into the buying and selling of stocks. Personally, when I open up my Yahoo Finance App in the morning, right around 9:30am, it is very interesting to see how equities "start their day." In general, stocks tend to be much more volatile in the beginning of the day, sometimes spiking in price before hitting a lull around 10:00am. While this volatility can lead to great returns, it can also lead to great losses. As talked about in the article, Should you Trade the First 30 Minutes?, an interesting perspective is discussed. Is the risk that comes with volatility worth it to trade in the morning?

When the stock market first opens, an enourmous amount of activity begins. Then trading slows, and due to a lack of volume, overtrading, and choppy price movements, prices tend to fall, before possibly gaining momentum in the latter part of the day (if you want to read more about trading during lunch time, check out this link <a href = "https://tradingsim.com/blog/9-reasons-why-i-do-not-trade-during-lunch/">here</a>).

In order to use data science to become more informed on whether the first 30 minutes of trading is worth the risk, we look to a set of data that we included in Part 1 - the intraday high. This data price point is the peak price for an equity during a single trading day. We wanted to find out how often the intraday high occurred in the first 30 minutes of trading.

We will focus on one of our equities, Immunogen.

The Dataset

In order to retrieve the type of intraday data that we needed, we were in search for minute-to-minute granularity. As a student in the Smith School of Business, and a member of the Quantitative Finance Society, I knew that finding the right dataset was likely an email away.

Luckily I was able to get my hands on the Wharton Research Data Services (WRDS) database from the Wharton School of Business at the University of Pennsylvania. This dataset is massive, supplying 49,000 corporate, academic, government and nonprofit users at 400+ institutions in 30+ countries with the data they need. The total size of this dataset is about 200 terabytes.

This immense amount of data, while cool to take in, was unweildy. The TAQ (NYSE Trade and Quote) allowed us to query millisecond tick data (the stock price). For a trading day that is from 9:30am-4:00pm, you can imagine how many milliseconds are recorded In order to rein in the data, I used pandas to reduce the millisecond data to minute-to-minute data, which is what we needed.

From there I did regular data cleaning processes, taking out NaN values and deleting duplicate rows for the same minute time. After the data was wrangled, we moved on to analysis.
